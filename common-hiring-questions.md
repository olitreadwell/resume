### Do you have experience mentoring or coaching other software engineers? What has been your experience? Why do you like mentoring or coaching other software engineers?

Yes, and it's been a consistent thread through my career, not a side activity.

At Hack Reactor, I taught full-time and part-time cohorts while building a mock interview app that let career services give feedback to far more students than one-on-one sessions allowed. That cohort ended up with a 90% graduation rate and 75% job placement within 90 days. At The Flatiron School, I ran a working group specifically on web accessibility and inclusive design. Getting those ideas in front of engineers early is worth more than retrofitting them later.

On engineering teams, I've treated mentoring as part of the job rather than optional. At WorkTango, I brought junior engineers into the technology decision-making process during the Rails-to-React re-architecture, which meant the team gained context they wouldn't have had otherwise. The speed and confidence of those engineers improved noticeably.

What I enjoy about it: there's a moment when someone solves a problem they were stuck on and the relief is visible. That moment compounds. The engineer is more confident next time, and the team gets faster as a result. That's not abstract. It's measurable if you pay attention.

My approach combines technical skill development, a socially responsible mindset, and soft skills like communication and emotional intelligence. I've found that engineers who understand why something matters, not just how to do it, make better decisions under pressure.

Outside work, I've organized tech community events (Refresh Austin, a11ySD) and contributed to open-source and Code for America projects. Being involved in the community is something I model because I think it matters, not just something I talk about.

### What does a good company culture look like to you?

My work in technology has focused on solving problems, collaborating with high-performing teams, building on actionable feedback, and delivering impactful incremental solutions. Let me break that down into the factors I actually look for.

**Mindful growth.** Technology sits on a difficult edge: move quickly without burning people out. Mindful growth means a clear understanding of business drivers, sustainable people practices, and industry best practices. I look for teams that treat investment in their peers as a way to drive the business forward. I check in with connections about day-to-day reality and retention before I apply.

**Empathetic communication.** Cate Huston, Engineering Manager at Automattic, wrote a piece about the five types of communication problems that destroy company morale. (https://qz.com/work/1587170/the-five-types-of-communication-problems-that-destroy-company-morale/) It stuck with me. Empathetic communication shows up in code reviews that aim to educate rather than just enforce, in meetings where people speak together rather than over each other, and in async discussions where context comes with the information.

**Methodical action.** A business has to make money to be sustainable, and I try not to forget that. I appreciate companies that integrate ongoing feedback from both quantitative and qualitative data. Transparent metrics on the goals behind my work let me innovate while understanding how my contribution connects to the organization's performance.

These factors come from a decade as an employee, with 8 of those years in high-velocity technology companies. I'm confident they're what build companies that attract, grow, and retain the kinds of teammates worth collaborating with.

### Describe a front end based project that you've worked on in a professional setting, and some of the challenges you experienced working on it.

At Condé Nast, I built a brand-agnostic paywall architecture along with a suite of components for over 26 media brands. The monetization goal was clear: drive a substantial increase in monthly subscriptions across the portfolio.

**Challenges:**

1. **Multiple brand integration.** The paywall system had to work seamlessly across 26 distinct media brands, each with its own design and user experience patterns. The answer was a shared core with per-brand configuration. Code in one place, decisions in twenty-six.

2. **Integration with Google Ad Platform.** Integrating with the ad platform meant ensuring ads displayed correctly on both sides of the paywall, and capturing accurate engagement metrics segmented by subscriber status. The analytics piece required careful instrumentation.

3. **Unified experience on Google AMP.** AMP's constraints on JavaScript and custom components made it tricky to keep the paywall consistent on AMP pages. We shipped a streamlined AMP version that stayed compliant while matching the core experience closely enough that users didn't notice the difference.

4. **End-to-end testing.** I authored a tech blog post about our approach to testing the paywall: [End-to-End Testing the Condé Nast Paywalls](https://web.archive.org/web/20211202210445/https://technology.condenast.com/story/end-to-end-testing-conde-nast-paywalls). We used Puppeteer to cover the flows that mattered most (subscribe, meter hit, hard gate) across multiple brands.

5. **Customizable rules to trigger paywall components.** We built a flexible rule engine allowing brands to configure when and how the paywall fired, based on user behavior, content type, or other criteria. Getting that right without degrading page performance or user experience was the hardest technical problem on the project.

6. **Cross-platform compatibility.** Readers came from every device and browser combination. Ensuring the paywall worked across all of them required systematic testing and a few targeted fixes for edge cases in older browsers.

The result: a year-over-year increase of over 100% in monthly subscriptions for Vanity Fair and Wired. The project taught me that iterative development, user-focused design, and rigorous testing aren't competing priorities. They're the same thing done right.
